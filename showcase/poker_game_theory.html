Poker Terminal · Game-Theory Lab ♠ ♥ ♦ ♣

There is no single best way to play poker. The optimal strategy flips with the number of players.

I built a Texas Hold'em engine, gave it eight named playing styles, and ran them against each other for hundreds of thousands of hands. The style that crushes heads-up is nearly the worst at a full table.

Eight archetypes — from the rock-tight Nit to the all-in Maniac — each a fixed set of parameters for how often to enter a pot, how hard to bet, and how much to bluff. No learning, no adaptation: pure strategies, so any edge comes from the strategy itself, not from out-thinking a specific opponent. That makes them a clean testbed for one question: is any style actually dominant?

The payoff matrix

01Who beats whom, one-on-one

Every pair of styles played 500 heads-up matches. Each cell is how often the row style finishes with more chips than the column style — green means the row wins, red means it loses.

One row is green almost all the way across: the Maniac. Relentless aggression wins heads-up because there's only one opponent to fold, and someone has to defend a blind every hand. Pure pressure is a dominant strategy here — so what happens if strategies reproduce in proportion to how well they do?

Evolutionary dynamics

02Let the winners breed

Start with an equal population of all eight styles. Each generation, every style's share grows or shrinks based on its average result against the current mix — the standard replicator dynamics of evolutionary game theory. Watch the ecosystem settle.

Because the Maniac beats everyone one-on-one, evolution collapses to a monoculture of Maniacs. Knock it out and the next-most-aggressive style inherits the table — a strict dominance ladder. Heads-up, aggression simply wins.

Then you seat eight of them at one table — and the story inverts.

The multiplayer twist

03Eight to a table, cash game

Same eight styles, now all seated together for 1,200 full-ring tables with rebuys. Ranked by profit per table (return on the starting stack). The heads-up hierarchy doesn't just weaken — it turns upside down.

The Maniac — unbeatable one-on-one — sinks to the bottom. With seven other players, someone almost always wakes up with a real hand, so relentless bluffing just donates chips. The patient Trapper and Tight-Passive, mediocre heads-up, win by folding the junk and letting the aggressors bleed each other dry. Table size is the game.

Tuning a single knob

04How much is too much?

If style is a set of dials, where are the optimal settings? Here one style's aggression and play-range are swept from tight to reckless against a fixed field, measuring profit at each setting.

Profit climbs with aggression, then the curve gets noisy near the top — there's a ceiling where more pressure stops paying. Play-range peaks in the middle: fold too much and the blinds eat you, play too much and you're in bad spots. Both dials have a sweet spot, and it isn't the extreme.

The takeaway. "Best" is meaningless without a context. A strategy that is provably dominant in one game structure can be provably terrible in another — the number of players alone reverses the entire ranking. That's the core lesson of game theory, made visible in a card game.
